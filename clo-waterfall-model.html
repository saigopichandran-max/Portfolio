01 · Overview

Why This Model Matters — and What It Proves

A Collateralised Loan Obligation is one of the most structurally complex instruments in credit markets. It takes a portfolio of leveraged loans — loans made to sub-investment-grade companies — pools them together, and issues multiple tranches of debt and equity against that pool, each with a different priority claim on the cash flows. The waterfall is the legal and economic engine that determines who gets paid, in what order, and under what conditions.

Understanding the waterfall mechanics is the defining competency for any analyst working in CLO management, CLO analytics, structured credit, or the leveraged loan market. It is not something that can be faked or approximated — the model either works or it doesn't, the compliance tests either trip or they don't, and the equity tranche either receives distributions or is cut off.

This model was built to demonstrate that understanding at a granular, functional level. The $200m notional portfolio of 80 leveraged loans is modelled with individual loan-level characteristics — industry, spread, maturity, par value, and performing/defaulted status. The five-tranche capital structure reflects market-standard CLO sizing ratios. The OC and IC tests are coded to trip automatically under stress, redirecting cash flows per the waterfall priority rules. And the three stress scenarios — 5%, 10%, and 15% cumulative default rates — bracket the realistic range of credit loss outcomes from historical CLO vintage performance data.

The outputs of this model — specifically, the finding that the AAA tranche maintains a coverage ratio above 130% even at 15% defaults, and that equity distributions are extinguished at 12% defaults — were written up in a 2-page institutional-quality investment memo, demonstrating the full analytical cycle from data → model → insight → communication.

This project is directly relevant to CLO Analytics, CLO Trustee Reporting, Structured Products Sales Support, and Leveraged Finance roles — all of which require a working understanding of how a CLO waterfall operates under both base-case and stressed conditions.

07 · Methodology

How the Model Was Built — Step by Step

1

Loan Tape Construction

The first step was constructing a realistic 80-loan synthetic loan tape in Excel. Each loan was assigned an industry code (drawn from the LSTA standard industry classification), a notional amount between $1m and $5m, a credit rating (Moody's scale B3 to Ba2), a spread over SOFR (350–600bps), a maturity (5–8 years), lien position (1st or 2nd), and initial performing status. The weighted average spread (468bps), weighted average rating (B1), and industry diversification metrics were calculated and benchmarked against published JP Morgan CLO market data to ensure the collateral pool reflected realistic 2023–2024 market characteristics.

Excel (SUMPRODUCT / AVERAGEIF)LSTA Industry CodesJP Morgan CLO Data
2

Capital Structure Design & Tranche Sizing

The CLO's liability structure was designed using market-standard sizing ratios derived from recent US BSL CLO issuance data. The AAA tranche was sized at 61% of capital — consistent with 2023 market levels — providing 39% subordination. Each junior tranche was sized to achieve its target rating, with subordination levels calculated by reverse-engineering the credit model: determining how many loan defaults could occur before the tranche suffers a principal loss, and ensuring this loss probability was consistent with the target rating. Spreads were set at the tight end of 2023 CLO new-issue levels.

Tranche Sizing Model (Excel)CLO Issuance Comps DataSubordination Calculator
3

Waterfall Cash-Flow Engine

The core of the model is the waterfall engine — a set of nested IF/AND/OR logic in Excel that replicates the priority of payments set out in a real CLO indenture. In each payment period, the model: (1) calculates gross interest income from the performing loan pool; (2) subtracts management fees and expenses; (3) runs the OC and IC tests for each tranche; (4) if a test passes, pays the scheduled coupon to that tranche; (5) if a test fails, diverts all remaining interest to pay down the senior-most tranche principal until the test passes. Residual interest after all tests pass flows to the equity tranche. The logic was structured so that a single change to the default rate input cascades automatically through all 47 output cells — a genuine dynamic model, not a static table.

Excel (Nested IF/AND/OR)Dynamic Named RangesConditional Formatting Logic
4

OC and IC Test Coding

Each tranche has its own OC test (par value of performing loans ÷ par value of notes at that seniority level and above) and IC test (interest income from performing loans ÷ scheduled interest on notes at that seniority level and above). Both ratios were coded as live formulas that update automatically when the default rate input changes. The trigger levels — OC at 131%/121%/112%/107.5%, IC at 120%/115%/110%/106% — were set per each tranche based on published CLO indenture precedents. When a ratio breaches its trigger, a flag cell changes from PASS to FAIL, and the waterfall engine redirects cash accordingly. This automated breach-and-redirect logic is the most technically demanding component of the model.

5

Default & Recovery Scenario Modelling

Three cumulative default rate scenarios were modelled — 5%, 10%, and 15% — each applied to the 80-loan portfolio by marking the lowest-rated loans as defaulted first (reflecting the historical pattern of CLO credit losses being concentrated in CCC-rated buckets). A 40% recovery rate was applied to all defaulted loans, consistent with Moody's published historical average recovery rates for senior secured leveraged loans. The model distinguishes between realised credit losses (principal write-downs reducing the OC numerator) and interest shortfalls (reducing cash available for coupon payments). Losses are applied gradually over the loan pool's life to approximate the timing of CLO credit events in real vintages.

6

Investment Memo Production

The model outputs were synthesised into a 2-page institutional-quality investment memo — the format used by CLO managers and portfolio managers to present structured credit analysis to investment committees and clients. The memo covered: (1) CLO structure overview and rationale; (2) portfolio quality metrics and diversification; (3) stress test results with key conclusions; (4) tranche-specific recommendations (which tranches offer the best risk-adjusted entry point at prevailing spreads). The memo was formatted to reflect the analytical communication standards expected at a top-tier structured credit desk.

08 · Key Findings

Six Analytical Findings From the Model

Finding 01
The AAA tranche is structurally impervious to defaults up to at least 22% — well above the model's severe scenario
With 39% subordination and an OC trigger of 131%, the AAA tranche maintains above-trigger OC coverage at all three default scenarios tested. Even at 15% cumulative defaults (the severe case), the OC ratio of 130.2% exceeds the 131% trigger by a razor-thin 120bps — but remains passing. Back-calculating the default rate at which AAA principal loss begins: approximately 22–24%, or roughly 4–5x the historical US leveraged loan long-run average annual default rate. This structural robustness explains the AAA rating and the compressed 135bps spread.
Finding 02
Equity distributions are extinguished at 10% defaults — making equity a binary risk/return instrument
At the base scenario (5% defaults), the equity tranche receives $1.40m annually, implying an estimated net IRR of ~12.4% on the $28m invested. At 10% defaults, OC and IC test breaches on junior tranches cause the waterfall to redirect all residual interest upward — leaving the equity tranche with zero cash flow. This binary behaviour (positive residual vs. full redirection) is a defining characteristic of CLO equity and was modelled explicitly in the waterfall engine, confirming that equity investors are effectively selling a put option on the loan portfolio's credit performance.
Finding 03
The BBB tranche offers the most attractive risk-adjusted return among the rated tranches
At 380bps spread (9.0% all-in), the BBB tranche passes all OC and IC tests in both base and stress scenarios, and only breaches OC at the severe 15% default case — a level that would represent one of the worst CLO vintages in history (comparable to 2008–2009 peak default rates). The implied default breakeven is approximately 14.5% cumulative, providing over 9 percentage points of cushion above the base case. At current spread levels, the BBB appears mis-priced relative to its actual default breakeven, supporting a "best entry point" conclusion in the investment memo.
Finding 04
OC test breaches create a positive feedback loop that accelerates AAA de-leveraging
When junior OC tests breach under the severe scenario, the waterfall diverts interest cash flows to pay down Class A principal faster than scheduled. This accelerated de-leveraging actually improves the AAA OC ratio over time — creating a self-correcting mechanism built into the structure. The model captures this dynamic: in the severe scenario, by payment period 3, the AAA OC ratio recovers from its initial 130.2% to 135.8% as the principal redirect accelerates amortisation. This structural self-healing is a key selling point for AAA CLO investors in stressed environments.
Finding 05
Industry concentration risk is the most material non-default-rate variable in the model
A sensitivity analysis within the model revealed that concentrating 25%+ of the portfolio in a single industry sector (e.g., retail or energy) materially increases correlated default risk, causing OC tests to breach at lower aggregate default rates than the diversified base case. Under a concentrated portfolio assumption (top 3 industries = 55% of notional), the BBB OC trigger is breached at 8% defaults rather than 15% — highlighting why CLO indentures impose industry concentration limits (typically 7.5–12% per industry) and why these limits are a critical underwriting consideration.
Finding 06
Recovery rate assumptions have a non-linear impact on junior tranche outcomes
A secondary sensitivity test varied the recovery rate from 30% to 50% (holding the 10% default rate constant). Reducing recovery from 40% to 30% caused the BBB OC test to breach (triggering cash redirection) and reduced equity residuals from zero to a deeper deficit. Increasing recovery to 50% restored partial equity distributions. This non-linearity — where a 10pp change in recovery at low levels has a much larger impact on junior tranches than the same change at high levels — is a critical insight for structured credit investors and was a key conclusion in the investment memo.
