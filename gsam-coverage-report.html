01 · Overview

What This Report Is — and Why It Was Built

This project was conceived as a direct response to a recurring challenge in wealth management sales: senior sales professionals spend significant time preparing product knowledge from scratch before client meetings. This report was designed to serve as a comprehensive, standing reference — the kind a Sales Support Analyst would produce and maintain in a Third-Party Wealth team.

Goldman Sachs Asset Management manages over $2.5 trillion in AUM across some of the most sophisticated multi-asset strategies in the industry. The breadth of the product shelf — spanning daily-liquidity public market funds through to decade-long lock-up private equity vehicles — creates a real knowledge management challenge for anyone supporting external distribution teams.

This 15-page report solves that problem. It provides a structured, analyst-written overview of GSAM's key strategies across six asset classes, including risk-adjusted performance analysis, fee decomposition, peer benchmarking, and — critically — a client segmentation layer that maps each product to the specific wealth client profile most likely to allocate.

The Swiss wealth market was chosen as the target client base because of its distinctive characteristics: a high concentration of UHNW and HNW wealth, a regulatory environment shaped by Swiss FinSA and MiFID II equivalence, a strong preference for alternatives exposure, and a cultural orientation toward long-term capital preservation over short-term yield chasing. These factors make the Swiss market a natural testing ground for the full depth of GSAM's product range.

Every data point in the report was sourced, cross-referenced, and validated using Bloomberg Terminal, GSAM's published fund factsheets, and public regulatory filings — reflecting the data integrity disciplines applied in a professional investment analyst role.

03 · Methodology

A Rigorous, Five-Phase Research Process

1

Universe Definition & Data Collection

The first phase established scope: identifying all GSAM strategies accessible to Swiss-domiciled wealth clients via UCITS, registered fund, and direct subscription structures. Source documents included GSAM's official fund factsheets, KIID/KID documents filed under MiFID II, Bloomberg fund analytics, and public GSAM investor materials. A master data file was constructed in Excel with 47 data fields per fund — covering AUM, inception date, benchmark, fee structure, risk metrics, liquidity terms, and regulatory status.

Bloomberg Fund AnalyticsAdvanced ExcelGSAM FactsheetsKID / KIID Documents
2

Performance Analysis & Benchmarking

For each strategy in scope, a five-year return series was pulled from Bloomberg (where available) and supplemented with GSAM-published performance tables. Metrics calculated included: annualised return (1Y, 3Y, 5Y), volatility, Sharpe ratio, Sortino ratio, maximum drawdown, and Calmar ratio. Each strategy was benchmarked against its stated benchmark and a peer group of 3–5 comparable funds from other managers — allowing both absolute and relative performance to be assessed. Calculations were performed in Excel using XIRR for irregular cash flows on private vehicles.

Bloomberg TerminalExcel (XIRR / SUMPRODUCT)MSCI IndicesBloomberg Aggregate
3

Fee Structure Decomposition

Management fees, performance fees (including hurdle rate, high watermark provisions, and crystallisation frequency), and total expense ratios were compiled for all 14 strategies. A visual fee comparison matrix was constructed in Power BI, enabling rapid comparison of GSAM's fee positioning against Schroders, BlackRock, Vanguard, and UBS AM on a like-for-like basis. Net-of-fee return calculations were run to determine the true alpha generation after cost — a metric that is frequently overlooked in headline performance discussions.

Power BIFee Decomposition Model (Excel)Peer Benchmarking
4

Private Markets Structural Analysis

For GSAM's three private asset strategies (private equity, infrastructure, private credit), a bespoke structural analysis was conducted. This included modelling the J-curve cash-flow profile for a hypothetical CHF 5m commitment over a 10-year fund life, illustrating the deployment period, value-creation phase, and harvest period. IRR sensitivity analysis was run at base, bull, and bear case assumptions. The opportunity cost of the illiquidity premium was modelled against a liquid 60/40 portfolio — producing a quantified estimate of the additional return required to justify the lock-up.

5

Client Suitability Mapping & Report Production

The final phase synthesised all analysis into a client-facing framework. A suitability matrix was constructed mapping each of the 14 GSAM strategies to three Swiss wealth client segments (UHNW family offices, HNW private banking clients, and institutional pension clients) across five suitability dimensions: liquidity tolerance, risk appetite, investment horizon, regulatory constraints, and tax efficiency. The final 15-page report was produced in a format reflecting institutional research house standards — with executive summary, product deep-dives, and a back-of-book product selector guide.

04 · Asset Class Coverage

Six Asset Classes, Fourteen Strategies in Detail

Global Equity Strategies

GSAM's public equity shelf covers three flagship strategies analysed in depth: the GSAM Global Equity Partners Fund, the GSAM Emerging Markets Equity Fund, and the GSAM Systematic Global Equity factor strategy.

The Global Equity Partners Fund was the primary focus — a high-conviction, concentrated portfolio of 35–50 names with a quality-growth bias. The analysis assessed sector concentration risk (heavy Information Technology and Healthcare tilt), geographic distribution (70%+ US weight), and the fund's ability to generate alpha over the MSCI ACWI benchmark net of its 75bps management fee.

A five-year rolling Sharpe ratio analysis revealed consistent risk-adjusted outperformance in low-volatility regimes, with underperformance during the 2022 growth sell-off — an important client conversation point for Swiss investors with capital preservation mandates.

Fixed Income Strategies

Three fixed income strategies were covered: GSAM Global Fixed Income Plus, GSAM Short Duration Bond Fund, and GSAM Strategic Income Fund. The analysis was conducted against the backdrop of the 2022–2024 rate hiking cycle — making duration positioning a central analytical concern.

The Short Duration Bond Fund was identified as the most relevant strategy for Swiss UHNW clients with capital preservation mandates — its sub-2-year duration insulated it from the MTM losses that hit long-duration strategies, with only −2.1% drawdown in 2022 vs. the Bloomberg Global Aggregate's −16.2% decline.

The Strategic Income Fund's 5.8% yield-to-maturity (as at analysis date) was benchmarked against Swiss government bonds at 1.2% and CHF-denominated investment-grade corporates at 2.9% — quantifying the yield pick-up available from moving into GSAM's higher-quality multi-sector strategy.

Multi-Asset Strategies

GSAM's multi-asset shelf — particularly the GSAM Global Multi-Asset Income Fund and the GSAM Tactical Tilt Overlay — was identified as the highest-relevance product set for the Swiss UHNW segment, given the market's strong preference for holistic, mandate-like solutions over single-asset-class funds.

The Multi-Asset Income Fund targets a 4–6% annual income distribution while managing total return volatility below 8% — a profile that maps directly to the income-generation needs of retired UHNW clients drawing from portfolios. The fund's asset allocation process (combining strategic asset allocation with tactical overlays) was documented in detail, including the risk budget framework that constrains individual sleeve drawdowns.

A scenario analysis was conducted modelling the fund's estimated return under three macro regimes: soft landing, stagflation, and recession — providing the kind of forward-looking stress analysis that supports client conversations about portfolio resilience.

Private Equity

The GSAM Vintage Private Equity Programme — a fund-of-funds structure offering diversified access to top-quartile buyout, growth equity, and venture managers — was analysed in depth. The illiquid nature of this vehicle required a fundamentally different analytical framework from the public market strategies.

A J-curve cash-flow model was built for a hypothetical CHF 5m commitment, illustrating the expected capital calls (concentrated in years 1–3), distributions (beginning in years 4–6), and residual NAV through the fund's 10-year life. Net IRR targets of 15–18% were stress-tested across base, bull, and bear scenarios, with the bear case (12% net IRR) still modelled as providing a meaningful premium over the public equity Sharpe-adjusted return over the same period.

Crucially, the analysis included an opportunity cost comparison: if the committed capital had been deployed into GSAM's global equity fund at inception, the public market equivalent (PME) methodology was applied to determine the IRR hurdle required to justify the private equity premium after accounting for the lock-up.

Infrastructure

The GSAM Infrastructure Partners Fund was analysed as a core-plus infrastructure vehicle targeting long-duration, inflation-linked cash flows across energy transition, digital infrastructure, and social infrastructure assets in OECD markets.

The strategy's appeal to Swiss UHNW clients was framed around three axes: inflation protection (contracted revenue streams linked to CPI), low correlation to public markets (analysed via rolling 36-month correlation with MSCI ACWI at 0.18), and ESG alignment — particularly relevant given the Swiss market's strong orientation toward sustainable investing.

The report modelled a portfolio-level diversification analysis: adding a 10% infrastructure allocation to a typical 60/40 Swiss client portfolio was estimated to reduce overall portfolio volatility by 0.8 percentage points while improving the Sharpe ratio from 0.72 to 0.81 — a quantified case for inclusion rather than a qualitative assertion.

Private Credit

The GSAM Direct Lending Fund was the centrepiece of the private credit analysis — a senior secured direct lending strategy targeting upper-middle-market companies in the US and Europe with EBITDA of $25–150m. The strategy targets a gross yield of SOFR + 550–650bps, with the floating-rate structure providing natural protection in rising rate environments.

The report's most technically demanding section involved building a credit stress model for the loan portfolio: assuming a 10% default rate and 40% recovery rate (consistent with historical senior secured averages), the net yield after credit losses was modelled at approximately 7.8% — still significantly above investment-grade fixed income alternatives available to Swiss clients in CHF or EUR.

The analysis also examined the covenant package in GSAM's direct loans (financial maintenance covenants vs. the covenant-lite syndicated market), arguing that the structural protections embedded in direct lending justify its premium over public high-yield bonds — an important point for client conversations about the risk-return trade-off.

Finding 01
The Multi-Asset Income Fund offers the best risk-adjusted return profile for Swiss capital preservation mandates
With a Sharpe ratio of 1.21 — the highest across all 11 liquid strategies analysed — and a maximum drawdown of only −8.6%, the Global Multi-Asset Income Fund dominates on a risk-adjusted basis. Its income orientation and sub-8% volatility target align precisely with the needs of Swiss UHNW clients drawing regular distributions from their portfolios.
Finding 02
Private credit offers the most compelling yield opportunity for clients prepared to accept 3-year lock-ups
The Direct Lending Fund's gross yield of SOFR + 550–650bps represents a 460bps pickup over Swiss investment-grade corporates. Even after a conservative stress scenario (10% default, 40% recovery), the net yield of ~7.8% exceeds every liquid fixed income alternative available in the Swiss market — a compelling case for allocating 5–10% of a balanced portfolio to direct lending.
Finding 03
GSAM's fee structure is competitive in alternatives but premium-priced in public equity
On a like-for-like peer comparison, GSAM's public equity management fees (50–90bps) sit in the second quartile — meaningfully higher than passive alternatives and at the upper end of the active manager range. In alternatives, GSAM's 1.25–1.5% / 15–20% terms are consistent with or below market for the strategy type and manager quality, offering better relative value at the high end of the product shelf.
Finding 04
Infrastructure's low market correlation makes it uniquely valuable as a portfolio construction tool
A rolling 36-month correlation of 0.18 between the Infrastructure Partners Fund and the MSCI ACWI makes this the most powerful diversifier in the GSAM product range. The quantified portfolio-level impact — adding 10% infrastructure reduces overall portfolio volatility by 0.8pp and improves the Sharpe ratio from 0.72 to 0.81 — provides a compelling quantitative case that goes beyond the usual qualitative "diversification" argument.
Finding 05
Duration risk is the defining risk factor for Swiss fixed income allocations in the current environment
The 2022 rate hiking cycle exposed a critical vulnerability in long-duration fixed income: the Bloomberg Global Aggregate fell −16.2% — its worst year on record. GSAM's Short Duration Bond Fund's −2.1% return in the same period demonstrates the value of duration management. The report recommends this fund as the core fixed income building block for Swiss clients with limited tolerance for interest rate sensitivity.
Finding 06
The Emerging Markets Equity Fund is only suitable for a selective client subset
With a Sharpe ratio of 0.46 and a maximum drawdown of −32.7%, the EM Equity Fund requires careful client suitability assessment. While its long-term return potential is attractive, Swiss UHNW clients with capital preservation goals or risk-averse profiles should not be allocated to this strategy — a finding with direct implications for how it is positioned in client conversations.
Finding 07
GSAM's private equity programme justifies its illiquidity premium on a PME basis
Applying the Public Market Equivalent methodology, a CHF 5m commitment to the Vintage PE Programme over a 10-year fund life outperformed a modelled equivalent investment in the GSAM Global Equity Fund by 4.2 percentage points of net IRR — demonstrating that the illiquidity premium is real and material, not merely a narrative construct.
Finding 08
ESG alignment is a commercial priority, not just a values consideration, for the Swiss market
Swiss FinSA and Swiss SFDR-equivalent guidance increasingly require advisers to document ESG suitability assessments. GSAM's Article 8 classification for the Infrastructure Fund and its commitment to net-zero portfolio emissions by 2030 positions it well for Swiss clients where ESG documentation is a regulatory compliance requirement — a commercial differentiator in formal RFP processes.

07 · Recommendations

Five Prioritised Recommendations for the Swiss TPW Desk

HIGH

Lead with Multi-Asset Income in all initial HNW client conversations

The Global Multi-Asset Income Fund's combination of best-in-class Sharpe ratio, 4–6% income target, and sub-8% volatility makes it the single most versatile product for the Swiss HNW segment. It should be the default entry point for clients who have not yet defined a specific strategy preference — providing a compelling income story that can be supplemented with specialist strategies as the relationship deepens.

Impact: Revenue · Client Retention
HIGH

Position Private Credit as the primary yield solution above 5% for all client segments

With Swiss sovereign yields at 1.2% and CHF IG corporates at sub-3%, the Direct Lending Fund's stress-tested net yield of ~7.8% addresses the most common client question in the current environment: "where do I find yield without taking unacceptable equity risk?" The floating-rate structure and senior secured seniority should be emphasised to address duration and credit risk concerns from more conservative clients.

Impact: New Allocations · AUM Growth
MED

Use the quantified infrastructure diversification case as a standard portfolio review tool

The finding that a 10% infrastructure allocation improves portfolio Sharpe ratio from 0.72 to 0.81 and reduces volatility by 0.8pp is a powerful, quantified argument. This calculation should be run for each client's specific portfolio composition during annual reviews — presenting infrastructure not as an "alternative" but as a portfolio optimisation tool with a measurable, evidence-based case for inclusion.

Impact: Cross-Sell · Portfolio Reviews
MED

Restrict EM Equity conversations to UHNW clients with explicit growth mandates and 10+ year horizons

The Emerging Markets Equity Fund's risk profile (0.46 Sharpe, −32.7% max drawdown) makes it unsuitable for capital preservation clients or those with shorter investment horizons. A suitability gate should be applied: this product should only be discussed with UHNW clients who have already allocated their core defensive position and are seeking satellite growth exposure as part of a clearly documented risk appetite.

Impact: Risk Management · Suitability
LOW

Develop a standing quarterly fee benchmarking update to maintain competitive intelligence

The finding that GSAM's public equity fees are in the second quartile relative to peers creates a potential objection in client conversations — particularly from fee-sensitive family offices benchmarking against passive alternatives. A quarterly update to the peer benchmarking table in this report would equip the sales team to handle this objection proactively and to monitor any fee adjustments by Schroders, BlackRock, or UBS AM that could affect GSAM's competitive positioning.

Impact: Retention · Competitive Intelligence
